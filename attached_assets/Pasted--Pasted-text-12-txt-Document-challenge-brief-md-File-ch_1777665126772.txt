Thought for 20s
Treat this as a stateful deterministic message engine, not an “LLM chat app.” The challenge brief says the bot must compose from four inputs — category, merchant, trigger, and optional customer — and return the next message, CTA, send-as identity, suppression key, and rationale. The testing brief also makes it clear the judge will push context over time and expects your bot to keep state across turns, while scoring decision quality, specificity, category fit, merchant fit, and engagement compulsion.

Build this in three layers. First, a context store that accepts /v1/context idempotently by (context_id, version) and keeps the latest category/merchant/customer/trigger snapshot alive for the whole test. Second, a conversation store keyed by conversation_id so /v1/reply can continue the exact thread. Third, a composer/router that chooses one trigger as the primary reason to send, then renders a message with category voice, merchant facts, and a single low-friction CTA. That matches the judge harness contract and the endpoint surface it validates.

Your strongest implementation pattern is: normalize → rank → compose → dedupe. Normalize each incoming context into a common internal schema. Rank possible triggers by urgency, recency, merchant fit, and suppression status. Compose with deterministic templates per trigger.kind first, then fill with merchant facts and category voice. Finally, suppress repeats using the provided suppression_key plus your own conversation-level dedupe so you do not spam the same merchant or customer. The design brief explicitly says the old system is fragmented and that a shared 4-context composition framework is the point of the rewrite.

For scoring, optimize in this order: specificity, then relevance, then compulsion. The brief repeatedly rewards real numbers, dates, local facts, and source anchors, and punishes generic messages. Use one merchant metric, one trigger fact, and one concrete offer or date in almost every send. For dentists, the category voice allows clinical terms and prefers service-plus-price patterns such as “Dental Cleaning @ ₹299” over vague discount language; the same pattern exists across other categories like salons, gyms, restaurants, and pharmacies.

The best “first strong flow” is usually dentists + research digest or dentists + recall reminder, because the dataset gives very clean merchant signals, offers, and trigger payloads. The case-study file shows what a perfect message looks like: a source-cited hook, a merchant-specific anchor like high-risk adult patients, one clear CTA, and no hype. That is the exact shape you should replicate in code.

A production-grade MVP stack for this challenge is simple: FastAPI, Pydantic models, Redis or SQLite for state, and a rule-first composer with a small template library. Do not start with a free-form prompt that tries to “understand everything.” Start with deterministic branching on trigger.kind, then make the body smarter with merchant facts and category voice.
